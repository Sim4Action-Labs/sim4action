The Problem

The decision-maker asks

"I manage a complex socio-environmental system.
Why do my interventions keep failing?"

What Makes These Systems Complex?

🔄 Feedback Loops

Effects circle back to amplify or dampen the original cause.

⏳ Time Delays

Causes and effects can be separated by months or years.

🔗 Interconnectedness

A change in one factor ripples across ecological, economic, and social domains.

💡 Emergence

System-level behaviours cannot be predicted from individual parts.

Result: Solving one problem often creates or worsens others. Traditional one-variable-at-a-time approaches produce unintended consequences.

Linear Thinking vs. Systems Thinking

❌ Linear Approach

One problem, one fix. Assume simple cause → effect. Ignore side effects and feedback.

Outcome: Policy resistance. Unintended consequences.

✅ Systems Approach

See the whole picture. Map causal connections. Account for feedback, delays, and side effects.

Outcome: Identify leverage points. Anticipate cascading effects.

A Different Lens

The decision-maker asks

"What does a systems approach actually look like?"

What Is a Causal System Map?

A visual model of cause-and-effect relationships. Two building blocks:

  • Variables (nodes) — factors that change over time
  • Relationships (edges) — directed links with polarity, strength, delay
graph LR
    A["Population"] -->|"same"| B["Demand"]
    B -->|"opposite"| C["Availability"]
    C -->|"same"| A
    D["Policy"] -->|"opposite"| B

A 4-variable map reveals a balancing loop (Population → Demand → Availability → Population) and a leverage point (Policy reduces demand proactively).

Relationship Properties

Each causal link carries three attributes:

Same Direction (+)

Source ↑ → Target ↑

Opposite Direction (−)

Source ↑ → Target ↓

Strength
Strong · Medium · Weak
Time Delay
Days · Months · Years

Strength determines how much influence flows along each link. Delay determines how long for the effect to materialise.

Understand the System

Diagnostics Lab

The decision-maker asks

"What drives my system? Where are the hidden dynamics?"

Reading the Network

The system map is rendered as an interactive force-directed graph:

  • Node colour = domain (ecological, economic, management, social)
  • Edge colour: green = same direction, red = opposite
  • Edge thickness = relationship strength
  • Arrows = direction of causal influence

Filters: Domain, polarity, strength, temporal scale, specific nodes and edges.

Feedback Loops

Closed causal chains where effects circle back. The platform detects and classifies all loops:

🔄 Reinforcing (R)

Amplifies change — drives growth or decline.

⚖ Balancing (B)

Resists change — drives toward equilibrium.

In our fishery: Fish Stock → Catch → Revenue → Effort → Fish Stock is a reinforcing loop. Harvest Quota → Effort → Fish Stock creates a balancing dynamic.

Design Interventions

Intervention Lab

The decision-maker asks

"What happens if I increase the harvest quota?
What are the side effects?"

Token Diffusion: The Idea

Drop a pebble into a pond. Watch the ripples spread. Token diffusion works the same way:

  • Inject tokens at an intervention node
  • Each token carries a + or − charge
  • Tokens travel along edges, routed by strength
  • Charge flips on opposite-direction edges
  • Time delays slow propagation realistically

Result: A dynamic picture of which factors are affected, the direction of change, and when effects arrive.

Strength Routing & Time Delays

Routing Probabilities

Outgoing edges compete for tokens by normalised strength:

Strong=3   Medium=2   Weak=1

One strong + one weak → 75% vs 25%.

Temporal Delays

Tokens spend simulation steps "in transit":

Days=5 steps   Months=10   Years=20

A quota change may take months to affect fish stocks.

Two Modes of Analysis

🎬 Scenario Mode

Single simulation, real-time visualisation. Choose probabilistic or deterministic, forward or backward.

Best for: exploring a specific intervention.

📊 Ensemble Mode

10–1000 runs, different random seeds. Produces statistical distributions.

Best for: robust decision-making with confidence intervals.

Outputs: Time-series charts, node/edge flow maps, controllability gauge, CSV export.

Advanced Capabilities

⬅ Backward Diffusion

Instead of "What happens if I change X?", ask "What drives Y?"

Reveals root causes and upstream leverage points.

🧬 Genetic Algorithm Optimizer

Given a limited budget, how to distribute tokens to maximise impact on a target?

Evolves optimal resource allocation over 50–200 generations.

Monitoring Lab

The decision-maker asks

"I can't monitor everything. What should I watch?"

Five Centrality Metrics

Graph theory quantifies the structural importance of each variable:

Degree

Count of direct connections.

Most causal links?

Betweenness

Frequency on shortest paths.

Bottlenecks or bridges?

Closeness

Average distance to all nodes.

Fastest system-wide influence?

Eigenvector

Connected to important nodes.

Linked to most influential parts?

Katz

Direct + indirect path influence.

Broadest total influence?

From Metrics to Monitoring Programs

Combining metrics identifies strategically important variables:

Leverage Points

High on multiple metrics — greatest shift potential.

Sentinels

High closeness — early warning of system change.

Bridges

High betweenness — cross-subsystem spread.

Hubs

High degree — proxies for subsystem activity.

Practical implication: Centrality identifies the minimum "sentinel" set for the best picture of system health at a fraction of the cost.
