The Challenge

Why socio-environmental systems demand a different way of thinking

What Are Socio-Environmental Systems?

Systems where human activities and natural processes are deeply intertwined — fisheries, water resources, urban ecosystems, agriculture, public health, climate adaptation.

🌳 Ecological

Species interactions, nutrient cycles, habitat dynamics, climate effects

💰 Socio-Economic

Markets, livelihoods, investment, community well-being, equity

🏛 Governance

Policies, regulations, institutions, management decisions, compliance

Why Are They Complex?

🔄 Feedback Loops

Effects circle back to amplify or dampen the original cause. The system has its own "memory" and "behaviour."

⏳ Time Delays

Causes and effects are separated by days, months, or years — making it hard to connect actions to outcomes.

🔗 Interconnectedness

A change in one factor ripples across ecological, economic, and social domains in unexpected ways.

💡 Emergence

System-level behaviours (collapses, regime shifts) cannot be predicted by studying individual parts.

The Problem with Linear Thinking

❌ Linear Approach

Address one problem at a time. Assume simple cause → effect chains. Ignore side effects and feedback.

Result: Solving one problem creates or worsens others. Policy resistance. Unintended consequences.

✅ Systems Approach

See the whole picture. Map causal connections across domains. Account for feedback, delays, and side effects.

Result: Identify leverage points. Anticipate cascading effects. Design robust, adaptive interventions.

The Approach

Participatory systems mapping brings diverse knowledge together into a shared causal model

Participatory Systems Mapping

Stakeholders — scientists, policymakers, community members, indigenous groups — co-create a shared model of how their system works.

  • Identify factors: Name the key variables across all dimensions
  • Define relationships: Describe causal links with direction, polarity, strength, and delay
  • Build the map: Integrate perspectives into a network of factors and relationships
  • Analyse & act: Use computational tools to explore, test interventions, and plan monitoring

Why participatory? No single expert understands an entire socio-environmental system. Participatory mapping integrates local knowledge, scientific evidence, and governance realities into a single, shared model — giving every voice a place in the system map.

Understand the System

Diagnostics Lab — Explore, filter, and diagnose the network structure

Interactive Network Visualization

The system map is rendered as a force-directed network graph. Every visual element encodes information:

  • Nodes = system factors (variables), colour-coded by domain
  • Edges = causal relationships, with arrow direction showing causality
  • Edge colour: green = same direction, red = opposite
  • Edge thickness = relationship strength

Filters available: Domain, relationship type (same/opposite), strength (strong/medium/weak), temporal scale (days/months/years), specific nodes, and specific relationships.

Relationship Properties

Each causal link has three key attributes:

Polarity

Same Direction (+)

Source increases → Target increases

Opposite Direction (−)

Source increases → Target decreases

Strength

Strong / Medium / Weak — magnitude of causal influence. Affects token routing probabilities in simulations.

Temporal Delay

Days / Months / Years — how long the effect takes to materialise. A policy change may take years to affect populations.

Feedback Loops

Closed causal chains where the effect of a variable circles back to influence itself. The platform detects all loops using depth-first search and classifies them:

🔄 Reinforcing (R)

Product of edge polarities is positive. Amplifies change — drives exponential growth or decline.

⚖ Balancing (B)

Product of edge polarities is negative. Resists change — drives the system toward equilibrium.

The balance of reinforcing and balancing loops determines overall system behaviour. Systems dominated by reinforcing loops are prone to runaway dynamics; those with strong balancing loops tend toward stability.

Cluster Detection

Community detection algorithms identify groups of tightly connected factors — often corresponding to subsystems or thematic groups.

Louvain Algorithm

Optimises modularity to find groups with dense internal connections and sparse connections between groups.

Girvan-Newman Algorithm

Iteratively removes the most "between" edges to reveal natural community boundaries.

Clusters help identify which parts of the system are most tightly coupled and where natural boundaries exist between subsystems.

Design Interventions

Intervention Lab — Simulate how changes cascade through causal chains

Token Diffusion: The Metaphor

Drop a pebble into a pond. Watch the ripples spread, interact, and reflect. Token diffusion works the same way:

  • Inject tokens at an intervention node
  • Each token carries a + or − charge
  • Tokens travel along edges, choosing paths based on strength
  • Charge flips on opposite-direction edges
  • Time delays slow propagation realistically

Result: A dynamic picture of how an intervention propagates — showing which factors are affected, the direction of change (positive or negative), and the timing of effects.

Polarity flipping is the key mechanism: two opposite-direction edges in sequence produce a net positive effect. Dimmed nodes are part of the network but not on the primary propagation path.

Strength-Weighted Routing & Delays

Routing Probabilities

At each node, outgoing edge strengths are normalised into probabilities:

Strong = 3   Medium = 2   Weak = 1

A node with one strong and one weak outgoing edge routes 75% of tokens via the strong edge.

Temporal Delays

Tokens spend a number of simulation steps "in transit" based on the edge's delay:

Days = 5 steps   Months = 10   Years = 20

This creates realistic lag between cause and effect propagation.

Scenario vs. Ensemble Analysis

🎬 Scenario Mode

Run a single simulation with real-time visualisation. Step-by-step control. Play, pause, rewind. Ideal for exploring and understanding a specific intervention.

📊 Ensemble Mode

Run 10–1000 simulations with different random seeds. Produces statistical distributions of outcomes. Accounts for stochastic routing. Ideal for robust decision-making and comparing strategies.

Results include: Time-series charts of node flows, edge flow visualisation, colour-coded network maps, controllability gauge, and CSV export for further analysis.

Monitoring Lab — Identify key indicators using network centrality analysis

Why Centrality Matters for M&E

Not all variables are equally important. Centrality metrics from graph theory quantify the structural importance of each node in the causal network.

Practical implication: Rather than monitoring everything (expensive, often infeasible), centrality analysis identifies the minimum set of "sentinel" variables that give you the best picture of overall system health.

Five Centrality Metrics

Degree

Number of direct connections (in + out).

Which variables have the most causal links?

Betweenness

Frequency on shortest paths between other nodes.

Which variables are bottlenecks or bridges?

Closeness

Average distance to all other nodes.

Which variables influence the system fastest?

Eigenvector

Importance through connections to other important nodes.

Which are connected to the most influential parts?

Katz

Total influence via all direct and indirect paths.

Which have the broadest total influence?

From Metrics to Monitoring Programs

By combining multiple metrics, you identify different types of strategically important variables:

  • Leverage points: High on multiple metrics — greatest potential to shift system behaviour
  • Sentinel indicators: High closeness/eigenvector — early warning signals of system-wide change
  • Bridge variables: High betweenness — reveal cross-subsystem spread of change
  • Hub variables: High degree — practical proxies for subsystem activity

The Comprehensive Analysis mode calculates all five metrics simultaneously and presents them in a sortable table, enabling multi-criteria prioritisation of monitoring variables.

Platform Demo Guide

Quick reference for navigating and demonstrating the SIM4Action platform

Suggested Demo Flow

  1. Landing page: Show the system catalogue, filtering by category, different views (cards, list, map)
  2. Launch a system: Click "Launch" on any system card to open the app
  3. Diagnostics Lab: Explore the network. Toggle domain filters. Find feedback loops. Run cluster detection.
  4. Intervention Lab: Initialise token diffusion on a key node. Run step-by-step. Show the results window with time series and node flow maps.
  5. Ensemble mode: Run multiple simulations. Show statistical distributions of outcomes.
  6. Monitoring Lab: Run centrality analysis (e.g., betweenness). Show the comprehensive view with all metrics side-by-side.
  7. Compare: Switch between labs to show how understanding → intervention → monitoring forms a coherent workflow.

Key UI Elements to Highlight

Lab Switcher

Three buttons at the top of the app: Diagnostics, Intervention, Monitoring. Click to switch context.

Left Sidebar

Contains all filters and controls specific to the active lab. Collapsible sections for different feature groups.

Live / Draft Toggle

Switch between the main and draft spreadsheets. Useful for showing work-in-progress system maps.

Results Window

Floating panel for simulation results: time series, node flows, edge flows, export.

System Image

Optional reference image of the system (e.g., a map, photo, or schematic) displayed alongside the network.

Node Tooltips

Hover over any node or edge to see its details: name, domain, definition, polarity, strength.
