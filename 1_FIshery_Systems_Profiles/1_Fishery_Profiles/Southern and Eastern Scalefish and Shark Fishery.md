# Fishery Systems Profile: Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) 

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) represents Australia's largest Commonwealth fishery by volume and one of its most complex multi-species fisheries. This profile examines the SESSF as a dynamic system to support causal mapping efforts for the Blue Economy CRC's Seafood Futures project, with special focus on climate adaptation pathways and Indigenous knowledge integration opportunities.

## Executive Summary

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) spans nearly half of Australia's Fishing Zone, extending from southern Queensland around to southern Western Australia. As a multi-sector, multi-gear, and multi-species fishery, the SESSF generated approximately \$86 million in gross value of production in 2019-20, representing 20% of Commonwealth fisheries' economic output[^15]. The fishery operates under a sophisticated management framework centered on output controls, primarily through Individual Transferable Quotas (ITQs) within a tiered Harvest Strategy Framework that adapts management responses based on information quality[^3].

The SESSF faces significant challenges, including climate-driven shifts in species distribution and abundance, with several key commercial stocks experiencing varying levels of depletion. Some stocks like Eastern Orange Roughy have shown recovery, while others remain depleted despite rebuilding strategies[^14]. The fishery's future sustainability depends on effective climate adaptation across production, market, and governance dimensions, with particular emphasis on managing shifting distributions of target species. Opportunities exist to enhance Indigenous knowledge integration within management frameworks, addressing both climate resilience and cultural recognition. The multi-species nature of the fishery creates both complexity in management and resilience against single-species failures, positioning it as a critical test case for adaptive management in Australia's changing marine environment.

### 2.3 System Diagram Description

The SESSF system centers around "Seafood Production" as its focal point, influenced by interconnected environmental, economic, regulatory and social variables. The system operates through several key feedback mechanisms: (1) Biomass-effort dynamics, where stock abundance affects catch rates, which influence fishing effort allocation and future biomass; (2) Economic-regulatory feedback, where profitability drives fleet dynamics and quota trading, which in turn affects management responses; and (3) Market-production relationships, where price signals influence targeting practices.

Climate change creates emerging feedback loops through shifting species distributions, altered productivity, and changing operational conditions. The management system responds through the tiered Harvest Strategy Framework, which employs different levels of precaution based on information quality[^3]. This adaptive framework creates a stabilizing feedback between scientific uncertainty and management conservatism. Indigenous knowledge systems represent an underutilized component that could provide additional understanding of long-term ecosystem changes and sustainable harvest practices.

The multi-species nature of the fishery creates both complexity in management but also resilience against single-species failures. This complexity requires sophisticated quota markets and strategic fishing tactics to optimize fleet operations across diverse species and areas. The system exhibits time lags between management interventions and biological responses, particularly for long-lived deepwater species like orange roughy.

## System Dynamics

### 5.1 Key Feedback Loops

| Loop Name | Variables Involved | Nature | Significance |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Biomass-Effort Cycle | Stock biomass → Catch rates → Fishing effort → Fishing mortality → Stock biomass | Balancing | Core dynamic determining sustainable harvest levels |
| Economic-Regulatory Feedback | Quota value → Fleet investment → Fishing capacity → Profitability → Quota demand → Quota value | Reinforcing | Drives long-term industry structure and capitalization |
| Market-Targeting Loop | Beach prices → Species targeting → Landed species mix → Market supply → Beach prices | Balancing | Influences production composition and value |
| Climate-Distribution-Effort | Ocean temperature → Species distribution → Fishing locations → Catch efficiency → Profitability → Fleet adaptation → Fishing locations | Reinforcing | Emerging loop driven by climate-induced changes |
| Assessment-Management Cycle | Data quality → Assessment uncertainty → Harvest control rule tier → TAC conservatism → Stock abundance → Data quality | Balancing | Formal mechanism linking information quality to management precaution[^3] |
| Social License Cycle | Environmental performance → Public perception → Regulatory pressure → Management constraints → Environmental performance | Reinforcing | Influences regulatory freedom and market access |

### 5.2 Time Delays and Lags

| Process | Typical Delay Period | System Implications |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Stock rebuilding response | 3-20+ years (species dependent) | Extended time between management action and biomass recovery, particularly for long-lived deepwater species[^14] |
| Assessment-management cycle | 1-2 years | Lag between data collection and resulting management changes |
| Fleet adaptation to changing conditions | 2-10 years | Capital investment cycle influences speed of technological adaptation |
| Market development for new species | 1-5 years | Time required to establish markets for newly targeted species |
| Climate-driven productivity changes | 5-20+ years | Long-term shifts in system productivity require adaptive management |
| Ecosystem response to fishing pressure | 5-20+ years | Complex food web effects manifest over extended timeframes |
| Indigenous knowledge integration | 5-10+ years | Building relationships and governance systems requires sustained effort |
| Policy reform implementation | 2-5 years | Institutional processes create delays in policy innovation |

| Alternative protein competition | Medium-Long | Medium | Differentiation strategy, unique attributes emphasis |
| Digital marketplace expansion | Short-Medium | Medium | Direct platform engagement, enhanced digital presence |
| Personalized nutrition trends | Medium | Low-Medium | Nutritional profiling of species, targeted marketing |

## Conclusion

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery represents a complex adaptive system facing significant challenges from climate change, market dynamics, and evolving governance requirements. The multi-species nature of the fishery provides both challenges in management complexity and opportunities for adaptation through species portfolio diversification. Climate-driven shifts in species distribution and productivity will require innovation across production, management, and market dimensions.

The fishery's sophisticated management framework, particularly the tiered Harvest Strategy approach, offers a foundation for climate adaptation but requires evolution to incorporate dynamic references points and more responsive decision processes. Significant opportunities exist to enhance Indigenous knowledge integration, which could provide valuable insights for both climate adaptation and cultural recognition.

The SESSF's future sustainability depends on its ability to navigate the dual challenges of rebuilding depleted stocks while adapting to changing environmental conditions. High-leverage intervention points include climate-responsive harvest strategies, dynamic spatial management, and enhanced market development for emerging species. The development of these adaptation pathways represents not only a challenge but an opportunity to transform this significant Australian fishery into a global model for climate-resilient seafood production.
