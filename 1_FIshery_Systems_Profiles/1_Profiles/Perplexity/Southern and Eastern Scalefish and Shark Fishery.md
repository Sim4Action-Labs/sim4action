# Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) Systems Profile

This profile presents a comprehensive analysis of Australia's largest Commonwealth fishery, the SESSF, with emphasis on causal relationships and system dynamics. The fishery faces significant challenges including climate-driven species redistribution, economic viability issues, and declining indicators for many species. This analysis focuses on identifying key system variables, relationships, and potential intervention points to support sustainable management and adaptation pathways.

### 2.3 System Diagram Description

The SESSF operates as a complex socio-ecological system where biological productivity, fishing effort, management interventions, and market dynamics interact through multiple feedback loops. The central focus is seafood production, directly influenced by stock biomass, fishing effort allocation across species and gear types, and environmental conditions.

Climate change is creating significant pressure through warming waters, particularly in the East Australian Current region, causing species redistribution and potential declines in overall productivity[^5]. The management system regulates production through TACs, which are set based on scientific assessment of stock status relative to reference points[^2]. However, the system faces challenges from under-caught TACs for many species, indicating economic or operational barriers to harvesting the allowable catch[^8].

Market dynamics show price flexibility in response to landed catch volumes, with evidence of market integration between different species and between the Sydney and Melbourne markets[^6]. Indigenous connections to Sea Country and marine resources represent a historically marginalized component of the system that is increasingly recognized for its importance to both cultural heritage and sustainable resource management[^4][^10].

## System Dynamics

### 5.1 Key Feedback Loops

| Loop Name | Variables Involved | Nature (Reinforcing/Balancing) | Significance |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Harvest-Stock Feedback | Stock biomass, Fishing effort, Catch, Future biomass | Balancing | Core feedback loop in fisheries; catches reduce biomass which affects future catches[^3] |
| Economic Viability Cycle | Catch rates, Operational costs, Profitability, Fleet capacity | Reinforcing | Declining catch rates raise costs, reducing profitability and leading to fleet contraction[^12] |
| TAC Adjustment | Stock assessment, TAC setting, Catch, Stock biomass | Balancing | Management system responds to stock changes by adjusting TACs[^2] |
| Market Price Response | Catch volume, Market price, Fishing incentives | Balancing | Higher catches reduce prices, potentially moderating fishing effort[^6] |
| Climate Adaptation | Species shifts, Target species, Management response | Reinforcing | Climate impacts require adaptation in both fishing and management approaches[^5] |

### 5.2 Time Delays and Lags

| Process | Typical Delay Period | System Implications |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Stock recovery | Years to decades | Overfished species like eastern gemfish show limited recovery despite management[^13] |
| Management response | 1-2 years | Annual TAC setting process creates lag in response to stock changes[^17] |
| Fleet adjustment | 2-5 years | Changes in fleet capacity lag behind economic conditions[^12] |
| Recruitment effects | 1-5 years | Impact of recruitment on fishable biomass depends on species growth rate[^3] |
| Climate impacts | Decades | Long-term shifts in species distribution and productivity[^5] |

### 5.3 Non-linear Relationships

| Relationship | Nature of Non-linearity | Threshold Points (if known) |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Stock-recruitment | Reduced recruitment at low stock sizes | Stock-specific but generally below 20% of unfished levels[^3] |
| Economic viability | Accelerating decline below profitability threshold | When net economic returns become negative[^12] |
| Ecosystem shifts | Potential regime shifts with persistent climate change | Unknown but possibly already occurring in southeastern Australia[^5] |
| Market price response | Disproportionate price impacts with large supply changes | Species-specific and market-dependent[^6] |
| Management intervention | Stepped responses based on tier system | Reference points (e.g., B20, B40, B48) trigger different management responses[^17] |

## Conclusion

The SESSF represents a complex, multi-faceted fishery system facing significant challenges from climate change, economic pressures, and management complexities. Key to its future sustainability is the development of adaptive management approaches that can respond to climate-driven shifts in species distribution and productivity. The current under-caught TACs across many species indicate structural economic issues that require attention beyond simple biological management.

Integration of Indigenous knowledge and recognition of First Nations peoples' connections to Sea Country remains underdeveloped but offers significant opportunities for enhancing management outcomes. Market dynamics play a crucial role in driving fishing behavior, with price flexibility and species substitution creating complex economic incentives that must be considered in management.

For the Blue Economy CRC's Seafood Futures project, the SESSF provides an excellent case study of the challenges facing Australian fisheries in a changing climate. Developing causal maps of this system will enable better identification of intervention points and adaptation pathways, ultimately supporting more resilient and sustainable seafood production into the future.
