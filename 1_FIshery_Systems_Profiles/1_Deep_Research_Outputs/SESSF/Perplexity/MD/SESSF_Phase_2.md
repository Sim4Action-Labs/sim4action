# Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery: Core Variables Analysis

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) represents one of Australia's most significant fishing operations, extending across a vast geographic area and encompassing multiple fishing methods, target species, and management approaches. This analysis identifies and documents the core variables that influence the SESSF system as part of the Blue Economy CRC's Futures of Seafood project, providing essential information for understanding system dynamics and developing effective management strategies for future sustainability.

## Executive Summary

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery operates within a complex and dynamic environment influenced by numerous variables across ecological, economic, social, technical, market, and indigenous knowledge domains. This analysis identifies and documents these core variables as part of the Blue Economy CRC's Futures of Seafood project. Environmental variables emerge as particularly significant, with south-east Australia identified as a global hotspot for ocean warming, experiencing sea surface temperature increases of 1.2°C since 1950 with further warming projected. These changes are already impacting species distributions and stock productivity throughout the fishery.

The measurement systems for these variables vary considerably in sophistication and frequency, from continuous satellite monitoring of environmental parameters to annual economic surveys and periodic social assessments. Data quality and availability differ markedly across variable categories, with environmental and economic variables generally having more robust and frequent measurements compared to social and indigenous knowledge variables, which often rely on qualitative assessments or less frequent data collection efforts.

Current baseline conditions reflect a fishery under significant environmental pressure, with several non-recovering stocks indicating potential systemic issues with management approaches or environmental changes exceeding adaptation capacities. Economic performance shows fluctuating Net Economic Returns (NER) in the Commonwealth Trawl Sector and varying profitability across different SESSF sectors, while social variables reveal declining fleet sizes and employment shifts due to industry consolidation and technological advancement.

The comprehensive mapping of these variables provides essential context for understanding the SESSF as a complex socio-ecological system, developing effective management strategies, and anticipating future changes in line with Blue Economy CRC objectives for sustainable industry development and technological innovation.

## 3.1 Variable Overview

The identification and documentation of core variables for the Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery involved a systematic examination of available data sources across six major categories: environmental, economic, social, technical, market, and indigenous knowledge variables. This categorization aligns with a systems approach to fisheries management that recognizes the complex interplay between natural, economic, and human dimensions of fishery systems. For each category, variables were selected based on their significance in influencing system function, their measurability, and data availability.

Environmental variables emerge as fundamental drivers of the SESSF system, with particular emphasis on oceanographic and climate indicators that influence species distribution, abundance, and ecosystem productivity. South-east Australia has been identified as a global hotspot for ocean warming, with sea surface temperature increases of 1.2°C since 1950 and further warming projected. Economic variables provide insights into the financial performance and viability of the fishery, with the SESSF contributing approximately \$86 million in gross value of production during the 2019-20 fishing season, representing 20% of Commonwealth fisheries' GVP.

Social variables capture the human dimensions of the fishery, including employment patterns and community dependencies, while technical variables document the operational characteristics that influence fishing effort and impact. Market variables reflect the commercial context within which the fishery operates, and indigenous knowledge variables acknowledge the cultural dimensions and traditional ecological knowledge relevant to the fishery.

Measurement systems for these variables vary considerably, from sophisticated satellite and oceanographic monitoring for environmental parameters to economic surveys, logbook reporting, and qualitative assessments for social and cultural indicators. Key data sources include the Australian Fisheries Management Authority (AFMA), Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation (CSIRO), Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics and Sciences (ABARES), Bureau of Meteorology (BOM), and various university research centers.

Baseline conditions established from these data sources indicate a fishery operating in a rapidly changing environmental context, with varying economic performance across sectors and ongoing social transitions related to industry consolidation. Notable is the prevalence of non-rebuilding stocks in the SESSF, suggesting that management approaches may not be sufficiently robust to address uncertainties or the impacts of climate change.

## 3.8 Conclusion

This analysis has identified and documented a comprehensive set of core variables influencing the Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery system across environmental, economic, social, technical, market, and indigenous knowledge domains. Environmental variables emerge as particularly significant drivers, with climate change impacts already evident in shifting species distributions and changing ecosystem productivity patterns. South-east Australia's status as a global hotspot for ocean warming, with sea surface temperatures having increased by 1.2°C since 1950 and further warming projected, represents a fundamental challenge for the fishery's future sustainability and economic viability[^7][^20].

The quality and availability of data vary considerably across variable categories. Environmental variables benefit from sophisticated monitoring systems including satellite observation, oceanographic measurements, and climate modeling, providing robust quantitative data for most indicators. Economic variables are generally well-documented through ABARES surveys and fishery statistics, though at a broader scale that may miss finer-grained impacts. Technical variables are reasonably well-measured through regulatory mechanisms such as logbooks and vessel monitoring systems. In contrast, social variables and particularly indigenous knowledge variables suffer from significant data limitations, with less frequent measurement, more qualitative approaches, and substantial gaps in systematic documentation.

Notable information gaps identified include: the socioeconomic impacts of fishery changes at the community level; fine-scale adaptation responses to climate change; comprehensive documentation of indigenous knowledge relevant to the SESSF; detailed understanding of supply chain dynamics and market relationships; and the cumulative effects of multiple stressors on the ecosystem. These gaps represent important areas for future research to support holistic fishery management in line with Blue Economy CRC objectives.

Based on this variables analysis, several recommendations emerge for improving monitoring and management approaches. First, there is a need to enhance social and cultural data collection to match the sophistication of environmental and economic monitoring. Second, variable monitoring should be increasingly integrated across domains to better understand system interactions and feedbacks. Third, climate adaptation should be more systematically incorporated into variable monitoring frameworks to support forward-looking management[^13][^17]. Fourth, indigenous knowledge variables should be developed in partnership with indigenous communities to ensure cultural appropriateness and mutual benefit. Finally, the non-recovery of some stocks despite management interventions suggests a need to reevaluate single-species Management Strategy Evaluations and develop approaches that better account for climate change impacts and ecosystem dynamics[^8][^13], supporting the Blue Economy CRC's aims for sustainable industry development and innovative management approaches.
