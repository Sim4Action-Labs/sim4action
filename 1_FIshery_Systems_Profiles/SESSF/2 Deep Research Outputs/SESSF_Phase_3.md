
# Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery: Phase 3 - System Dynamics Analysis

## Executive Summary

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) operates as a complex socio-ecological system characterized by multiple interconnected relationships across environmental, economic, management, and social domains. Key dynamics include interactions between fishing mortality and stock abundance, climate-induced shifts in species distribution and productivity, market forces influencing fishing effort, and ecosystem reorganization affecting stock recovery. Of particular concern is the persistent non-recovery of several stocks despite management interventions, suggesting that current single-species approaches may not capture critical ecosystem interactions or cumulative impacts of multiple stressors.

Several major feedback mechanisms operate within the SESSF, including balancing loops associated with stock-recruitment relationships and management responses to declining abundance, as well as reinforcing loops connected to economic viability, technological advancement, and climate-induced distributional shifts. These feedback mechanisms are complicated by significant time delays between management interventions and biological responses, creating challenges for adaptive management.

Critical thresholds within the system include biological reference points (B20, B40, B48), economic viability thresholds triggering industry consolidation, and ecological tipping points associated with climate change. The current status of several stocks relative to these thresholds suggests the SESSF may be approaching critical transitions that could permanently alter system structure and function.

Management implications highlight the need to transition from traditional single-species approaches toward more integrated ecosystem-based fisheries management. Developing multi-species harvest strategies, incorporating climate adaptation mechanisms, and integrating socioeconomic factors into management frameworks represent important steps toward addressing system complexity. Explicitly accounting for feedback mechanisms, time delays, and potential threshold effects in harvest strategy design could substantially improve management outcomes and system resilience in the face of increasing environmental and economic pressures.

### 3.1 Dynamics Overview

The system dynamics approach provides a framework for understanding the SESSF as a complex adaptive system characterized by interconnected components, feedback mechanisms, time delays, and nonlinear responses. This approach recognizes that fishery behavior emerges from interactions between ecological processes (recruitment, growth, mortality), human activities (fishing effort, market decisions), governance systems (management interventions, compliance mechanisms), and external drivers (climate change, international trade).

Key relationship categories within the SESSF include ecological connections (stock-recruitment relationships, predator-prey interactions, habitat associations), economic linkages (price-quantity relationships, market competition dynamics, industry profitability factors), governance interactions (management responses to stock status, compliance with regulations), technological relationships (fishing efficiency impacts, gear selectivity effects), and social-cultural connections (community dependencies, traditional knowledge systems).

Evidence quality varies substantially across these relationship categories, with stronger data supporting ecological relationships for key commercial species and economic patterns for major market segments. The SESSF benefits from extensive monitoring programs including logbooks, catch disposal records, vessel monitoring systems, observer coverage, and independent surveys. However, evidence quality diminishes for ecosystem interactions, social dynamics, and indigenous knowledge systems. The most robust evidence comes from AFMA, CSIRO, and ABARES, which maintain long-term datasets on catch, effort, biological parameters, and economic performance.

System stability factors include management interventions through the harvest strategy framework, natural density-dependent processes regulating population dynamics, economic feedback mechanisms adjusting fishing effort in response to profitability, and social adaptation strategies in fishing communities. However, these stabilizing mechanisms are increasingly challenged by climate change impacts, with south-east Australia identified as a global hotspot for ocean warming.

Critical uncertainties affecting SESSF dynamics include the extent and rate of climate change impacts on different species and ecosystems, complex ecological interactions potentially preventing stock recovery despite reduced fishing pressure, market dynamics and import competition effects on domestic prices, and the efficacy of current management approaches in addressing multi-faceted challenges.

### 3.8 Conclusion

The system dynamics analysis of the Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery reveals a complex network of relationships, feedback loops, time delays, and thresholds that collectively determine fishery behavior and outcomes. Key dynamics include the interactions between fishing mortality and stock abundance, climate-induced shifts in species distribution and productivity, market forces influencing fishing effort and targeting practices, and ecosystem reorganization affecting stock recovery. Of particular concern is the persistent non-recovery of several stocks despite management interventions, suggesting that the current single-species approach may not be capturing critical ecosystem interactions or the cumulative impacts of multiple stressors.

Evidence quality varies substantially across different aspects of the system, with stronger quantitative data supporting core biological and economic relationships, while ecosystem interactions, social dynamics, and indigenous knowledge components remain less well documented. This evidence imbalance creates challenges for integrated management, as decisions may overweight well-quantified direct impacts while underestimating less visible but potentially significant indirect effects and cross-system interactions. The development of ecosystem indicators and models represents an important step toward addressing these evidence gaps, but substantial work remains to integrate these approaches into operational management frameworks.

Significant knowledge gaps identified through this analysis include: the complex ecological interactions potentially preventing stock recovery; the specific mechanisms through which climate change affects different species and life stages; the socioeconomic impacts of fishery changes at the community level; the dynamics of market substitution between domestic and imported products; and the potential contributions of indigenous knowledge systems to sustainable fishery management. Additionally, there is limited understanding of how multiple feedback loops interact to produce emergent system behavior, particularly under novel climate conditions that push the system outside historical ranges.

Recommendations for system monitoring and management include: developing more integrated data collection approaches that can track variables across environmental, economic, and social domains; implementing adaptive management frameworks explicitly designed to account for feedback mechanisms and time delays; incorporating ecosystem indicators into harvest strategy design and evaluation; expanding climate adaptation planning to address distributional shifts and productivity changes; and creating more inclusive governance processes that can integrate diverse knowledge systems, including indigenous perspectives. The transition from single-species to multi-species harvest strategies currently underway represents an important step toward more holistic management but should be expanded to incorporate broader ecosystem considerations and explicit recognition of system dynamics principles.
