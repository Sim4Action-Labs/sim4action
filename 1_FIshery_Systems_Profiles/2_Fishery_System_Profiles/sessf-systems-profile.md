# Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery: Integrated Systems Profile

## Executive Summary

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) represents Australia's largest Commonwealth fishery by volume and one of its most economically significant fishing operations, contributing approximately $86 million in gross value of production (2019-20) and accounting for 20% of Commonwealth fisheries' GVP. This complex multi-species, multi-gear, and multi-jurisdictional system spans almost half of Australia's Fishing Zone, employing diverse fishing methods across several sectors including the Commonwealth Trawl Sector, Great Australian Bight Trawl Sector, and the Gillnet, Hook and Trap Sector. The fishery's significance extends beyond economic measures to include important social, cultural, and ecological dimensions within Australia's seafood industry.

The SESSF operates within a rapidly changing environment, with south-east Australia identified as a global hotspot for ocean warming, experiencing sea surface temperature increases of 1.2°C since 1950 with further warming of 0.3-1.2°C projected by 2040. These environmental changes are driving significant shifts in species distributions and stock productivity, creating complex challenges for management. System dynamics analysis reveals multiple interconnected relationships across environmental, economic, management, and social domains, with feedback mechanisms operating at various time scales. Of particular concern is the persistent non-recovery of several stocks despite management interventions, suggesting current single-species approaches may not adequately capture critical ecosystem interactions or cumulative impacts of multiple stressors.

Future pathways for the SESSF focus on enhancing adaptive capacity through improved monitoring, flexible management approaches, technological innovation, and cross-jurisdictional coordination. Climate adaptation strategies include developing climate-adjusted stock assessment models, implementing dynamic spatial management, and identifying climate refugia for vulnerable species. Management interventions prioritize transitioning toward more integrated ecosystem-based approaches through multi-species harvest strategies, cross-jurisdictional coordination mechanisms, and pre-season forecasting to inform quota-setting. These adaptations must be complemented by technological innovations in remote electronic monitoring, advanced gear selectivity, and predictive fishing tools that utilize oceanographic data.

The SESSF's market position is primarily focused on domestic fresh fish supply, particularly to markets in southeast Australia, with limited export potential due to relatively small catch volumes. While the fishery faces competitive pressures from imports, aquaculture production, and changing consumer preferences, opportunities exist to strengthen its position through value-adding, sustainability certification, and market diversification. The value chain analysis highlights the need for improved efficiency and integration across all components, from harvesting to retail, with particular emphasis on enhancing data collection, processing capabilities, and market responsiveness.

Implementation priorities for the SESSF must balance immediate climate adaptation needs with longer-term structural reforms in management approaches and industry practices. Key priorities include implementing climate-adjusted stock assessments, developing multi-species harvest strategies, enhancing market responsiveness through data analytics, establishing cross-jurisdictional coordination mechanisms, and investing in value-adding processing capabilities. Success depends on building adaptive capacity across the fishing industry, management institutions, and dependent communities, supported by strong governance arrangements, transparent stakeholder engagement, robust scientific information, and adequate financial resources. Through these integrated approaches, the SESSF can enhance its resilience and continue to provide sustainable economic, social, and cultural benefits in the face of increasing environmental and market challenges.

## 3.1 System Foundation

### 3.1.1 Fishery Overview

The Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery (SESSF) has evolved significantly since its early development, transitioning through various management approaches to become Australia's most valuable Commonwealth-managed fishery. The fishery expanded substantially with technological advancements in the mid-20th century, leading to concerns about sustainability that prompted the implementation of more structured management approaches by the 1980s. Currently, the SESSF encompasses multiple sectors across a vast geographic area, with a gross value of production of $86 million in 2019-20, accounting for approximately 20% of Commonwealth fisheries' GVP.

The SESSF employs diverse fishing methods including demersal otter trawl, Danish seine, demersal longline, and gillnet operations targeting different species across several sectors. The Commonwealth Trawl Sector primarily targets tiger flathead, pink ling, and blue grenadier; the Great Australian Bight Trawl Sector focuses on Bight redfish and deepwater flathead; while the Gillnet, Hook and Trap Sector mainly targets gummy shark, blue eye trevalla, and pink ling. This operational diversity contributes to the complexity of managing the fishery as a sustainable resource while adapting to changing environmental conditions.

Critical challenges facing the SESSF include adapting to climate change impacts, addressing sustainability issues for certain species such as school shark, navigating complex jurisdictional boundaries, responding to changing market dynamics including import competition, and balancing economic viability with environmental sustainability objectives. The fishery's management is primarily through output controls such as Total Allowable Catches (TACs), which are distributed among fishers as individual transferable quotas, supplemented by input controls including limited entry, gear restrictions, spatial closures, and prohibited species designations.

### 3.1.2 Geographic Scope

The SESSF encompasses a vast maritime area extending from Fraser Island in Queensland south along the eastern coast and west to Cape Leeuwin in Western Australia. This expansive region covers nearly half of Australia's Fishing Zone, making it one of the largest managed fisheries in Australian waters. The physical extent includes continental shelf and slope waters, with fishing operations conducted in both shallow coastal zones and deeper offshore waters exceeding 700 meters in depth.

Key fishing regions within the SESSF are differentiated by sector, with the Commonwealth Trawl Sector primarily operating off New South Wales, eastern Victoria, and Tasmania; the Great Australian Bight Trawl Sector functioning in the waters of the Great Australian Bight; and the Gillnet, Hook and Trap Sector operating across southern waters with particular concentration in Bass Strait and Tasmanian waters. The fishery utilizes over 20 ports across this extensive range, with major landing sites including Ulladulla, Lakes Entrance, Portland, Eden, Hobart, Adelaide, San Remo, and Port Lincoln.

The geographic scope also incorporates diverse marine ecosystems characterized by dynamic oceanographic features including the East Australian Current and the Leeuwin Current. These systems support complex food webs and habitats ranging from rocky reefs to soft sediment environments that form the ecological foundation for the fishery's target species. Additionally, the SESSF incorporates traditional sea country boundaries of indigenous Australian communities, adding a cultural dimension to the geographic delineation of the fishery.

### 3.1.3 Stakeholder Landscape

The SESSF involves a complex network of stakeholders with varying interests, influences, and dependencies. Government and management entities hold primary responsibilities, with the Australian Fisheries Management Authority (AFMA) serving as the principal management body at the Commonwealth level. State agencies including the NSW Department of Primary Industries, Victorian Fisheries Authority, South Australian Department of Primary Industries and Regions, and Tasmanian Department of Natural Resources and Environment manage complementary jurisdictions and resources that interact with the SESSF. Management Advisory Committees (MACs) and Resource Assessment Groups (RAGs) bring together managers, scientists, industry representatives, and other stakeholders to develop advice on management measures.

The commercial fishing industry comprises diverse participants including vessel operators varying in scale from smaller owner-operated vessels to larger corporate entities, processors transforming harvested products for various market segments, marketing organizations representing collective interests, and numerous supply chain participants extending beyond immediate harvesting operations. Research organizations and academic institutions including CSIRO's Marine and Atmospheric Research Division, ABARES, and university research centers conduct critical research informing management decisions.

Conservation interests are represented by environmental groups such as the Australian Marine Conservation Society, marine parks administration, conservation agencies, and indigenous custodians bringing unique perspectives grounded in traditional connections to sea country. The broader community includes coastal communities with historical identities shaped by fishing activities, recreational fishers interacting with commercial operations, seafood consumers representing the ultimate market for SESSF products, tourism operators, and the general public with interests in marine conservation and cultural heritage. This diverse stakeholder landscape creates complex governance challenges requiring balanced consideration of multiple perspectives, values, and knowledge systems.

### 3.1.4 Management Framework

The SESSF operates within a comprehensive management framework established primarily by the Australian Fisheries Management Authority (AFMA) under the Commonwealth Fisheries Harvest Strategy Policy. This framework employs a combination of output controls, primarily Total Allowable Catches (TACs) distributed as individual transferable quotas, and input controls including limited entry, gear restrictions, and spatial closures. The management system is designed to achieve biological, economic, and ecological objectives while accommodating the multi-species, multi-gear nature of the fishery.

The regulatory environment is complex, spanning Commonwealth, state, and international jurisdictions. At the federal level, the fishery is primarily governed by the Fisheries Management Act 1991, with additional federal legislation including the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 imposing further requirements related to environmental sustainability and threatened species protection. The fishery encompasses waters managed under various Offshore Constitutional Settlement arrangements with state governments, creating cross-jurisdictional complexity with fishing operations potentially subject to different regulatory requirements depending on their precise location and target species.

Decision-making processes occur through formal consultative structures including Management Advisory Committees and Resource Assessment Groups, which provide forums for diverse stakeholder perspectives to influence management approaches. Compliance systems include mandatory logbook reporting, catch disposal records, vessel monitoring systems, observer programs, port sampling, and fishery-independent surveys. International obligations significantly influence SESSF management, with Australia committed to numerous agreements including the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, the UN Fish Stocks Agreement, and various regional fisheries management arrangements reflected in domestic policies.

## 3.2 System Variables

### 3.2.1 Environmental Variables

The SESSF operates within a rapidly changing marine environment, with south-east Australia identified as a global hotspot for ocean warming. Sea surface temperature has increased by 1.2°C since 1950, with further increases of 0.3-1.2°C projected by 2040. This warming trend is accompanied by increasing ocean acidification, declining oxygen levels, changing current patterns, and more frequent marine heatwaves. These environmental shifts are already contributing to declines in several SESSF stocks, including eastern jackass morwong, blue warehou, and gemfish, while potentially benefiting others such as flathead, which may increase in abundance by approximately 5%.

Oceanographic conditions play a crucial role in shaping ecosystem productivity and species distributions within the SESSF. Major currents affecting the region include the East Australian Current, which is strengthening with climate change, and the Leeuwin Current, which weakens during El Niño events. Upwelling events, particularly the Bonney upwelling, are critical for ecosystem productivity but are occurring later each year due to climate influences. Climate patterns including the Southern Oscillation Index and Southern Annular Mode affect these oceanographic features, creating complex environmental dynamics that directly impact fishery productivity.

Environmental monitoring within the SESSF region is relatively robust compared to other variable categories, with continuous satellite observation, oceanographic measurements, and climate modeling providing quantitative data for most indicators. However, gaps remain in understanding fine-scale environmental impacts on specific species and habitats, particularly regarding cumulative effects and ecosystem-level responses to multiple stressors. Enhanced monitoring capacity, including expanded use of in-situ sensors and integrated ecosystem observations, will be critical for supporting adaptive management in the face of ongoing environmental change.

### 3.2.2 Economic Variables

Economic variables provide critical insights into the financial performance and viability of the SESSF, with significant implications for management decisions and industry strategies. Economic performance varies considerably across sectors, with fluctuating Net Economic Returns (NER) in the Commonwealth Trawl Sector and varying profitability in other SESSF sectors. These economic indicators are influenced by multiple factors including operational costs, market dynamics, regulatory constraints, and environmental conditions.

Operating costs represent a significant factor affecting profitability, with fuel, labor, and maintenance expenses varying by sector and fishing method. Fleet capacity has decreased substantially over time, with trawl operations decreasing by approximately 60% between 2000 and 2021 due to industry consolidation, technological advancement, and changes in management approaches. This consolidation has implications for economic efficiency, market structure, and community impacts throughout the SESSF region. Quota values reflect expected profitability and resource access rights, providing an important indicator of industry confidence in the long-term sustainability and economic viability of different fishery components.

## 3.3 System Dynamics

### 3.3.1 Core Relationships

The SESSF operates as a complex socio-ecological system characterized by multiple interconnected relationships across environmental, economic, management, and social domains. Core relationships include the fundamental interactions between fishing mortality and stock abundance, with higher fishing pressure generally leading to reduced population levels. This relationship is complicated by environmental factors, particularly climate change, which is driving shifts in species distribution and productivity throughout the SESSF region. Research indicates variable responses across species, with some stocks declining due to changing conditions while others may benefit from warming waters, creating complex management challenges.

Market forces significantly influence fishing behavior, with price signals affecting effort allocation, targeting practices, and investment decisions. Higher prices for particular species or size classes typically stimulate increased targeting, though this relationship is moderated by quota constraints, operational factors, and alternative fishing opportunities. Import competition exerts substantial pressure on domestic prices, with research demonstrating negative impacts on prices of wild-caught species in Australian markets, particularly for lower-valued species, affecting overall industry profitability and structure.

Ecosystem properties have complex relationships with stock dynamics, including predator-prey interactions, habitat associations, and competitive relationships. These ecosystem dimensions appear particularly significant for understanding the persistent non-recovery of several SESSF stocks despite reduced fishing pressure, suggesting that altered ecosystem structures may be preventing population rebuilding. Management interventions through harvest strategies aim to control fishing mortality and promote stock sustainability, though the effectiveness of these approaches varies across species and circumstances, highlighting the importance of adaptive management approaches that can respond to emerging evidence and changing conditions.

### 3.3.2 Feedback Mechanisms

Several major feedback mechanisms operate within the SESSF, creating dynamic system behavior that can either amplify or moderate changes over time. Stock-recruitment feedback represents a fundamental reinforcing loop, where stock depletion leads to reduced recruitment, potentially driving further population decline if not addressed through management intervention. This biological feedback operates alongside the management response cycle, a balancing loop where stock declines trigger reduced TACs, leading to decreased fishing mortality and eventual stock recovery. These complementary feedback mechanisms form the basis for sustainable fisheries management, though their effectiveness depends on accurate stock assessment, appropriate harvest control rules, and effective implementation.

Economic viability forms another important feedback loop, where declining catches can reduce profitability, driving fleet consolidation and increased efficiency among remaining operators, potentially leading to higher catch rates for individual vessels even as overall fleet size decreases. Climate-induced distributional shifts create complex feedbacks, with ocean warming altering species distributions, changing fishing efficiency and patterns, potentially reinforcing further ecosystem changes. Ecosystem reorganization loops involve changes in trophic structure due to fishing pressure, altered predation and competition relationships, and potential impediments to stock recovery despite reduced harvesting, highlighting the complex ecological dimensions of fishery systems.

These feedback mechanisms are complicated by significant time delays between management interventions and biological responses, creating challenges for adaptive management. The time required for stocks to respond to reduced fishing pressure varies from several years to decades, with some species showing no recovery despite long-term protection. Similarly, economic signals may take years to translate into industry restructuring, while ecosystem reorganization can occur over extended timeframes. Understanding and accounting for these feedback dynamics and associated time delays is critical for developing effective management approaches that can anticipate system behavior and avoid unintended consequences of intervention strategies.

### 3.3.3 Time Delays

Time delays represent a critical aspect of SESSF dynamics, creating challenges for management and adaptation. The annual management cycle involves delays between stock assessment, management response, and implementation, typically requiring about 1 year to complete. More significant are the delays between management implementation and biological response, which can range from 3-10+ years depending on species life history, environmental conditions, and management measure effectiveness. These biological delays are particularly evident in recovery timeframes for overfished stocks, which may require 5-20+ years to rebuild to target levels, with some stocks showing no recovery despite long-term protection.

Recruitment to fishable biomass involves species-specific delays of 1-5 years, representing the time required for new year classes to grow to harvestable size. Climate change impacts on species distribution also involve significant delays of 5-20+ years, as populations gradually shift in response to changing environmental conditions. Economic adjustments similarly involve delays, with fleet capacity typically requiring 2-5 years to respond to profitability changes, reflecting the capital-intensive nature of fishing businesses and regulatory constraints on entry and exit.

These various time delays interact to create complex system behavior, with interventions in one domain potentially having delayed and sometimes unexpected consequences in others. For example, management measures intended to rebuild stocks may have immediate economic impacts while biological benefits materialize only after several years. Understanding these temporal dynamics is essential for setting realistic expectations, developing appropriate transitional support mechanisms, and designing management approaches that can accommodate inherent system delays. Improved monitoring and forecasting capabilities can help reduce some management delays, but fundamental biological and ecological timeframes will continue to create inherent challenges for fishery governance.

### 3.3.4 System Thresholds

The SESSF operates with several critical thresholds that can trigger significant changes in system behavior or management response. Biomass reference points, including B20 (limit reference point), B40 (intermediate reference point), and B48 (target reference point for maximum economic yield), serve as key biological thresholds that trigger different management responses. Currently, multiple stocks are below target levels, and some are below limit reference points, indicating significant sustainability concerns. These biological thresholds are complemented by catch/TAC trigger levels that prompt reassessment when harvest reaches 75% of TAC for low-risk species, reflecting an approach to balancing assessment costs with management needs.

Economic viability thresholds are less explicitly defined but manifest in industry behavior, with declining profitability triggering consolidation, disinvestment, or operational changes when returns fall below acceptable levels. This economic dimension interacts closely with biological thresholds through the management system, as reduced TACs in response to biological concerns can exacerbate economic pressures. Climate temperature thresholds represent an emerging concern, with evidence suggesting that certain temperature ranges may trigger significant changes in species distribution, recruitment success, or ecosystem function, though specific thresholds remain poorly defined for many species.

Ecosystem trophic reorganization points represent potential ecological thresholds beyond which system structure and function may fundamentally change, creating new stable states that resist return to previous conditions. Evidence for such reorganization exists for some non-recovering species, suggesting that reduced fishing pressure alone may be insufficient to rebuild stocks once certain ecological thresholds have been crossed. Management trigger points for declaring stocks as overfished or subject to overfishing serve as governance thresholds that activate more intensive management interventions, including formal rebuilding strategies. Understanding and monitoring these various thresholds is essential for anticipating system changes, developing appropriate intervention strategies, and avoiding irreversible shifts in system state.

Climate adaptation represents a critical priority for the SESSF, given south-east Australia's status as a global hotspot for ocean warming. Enhanced monitoring of oceanographic conditions and species distributions forms an essential foundation for adaptation, enabling the early detection of changes and informing management responses. This monitoring must be coupled with the development of climate-adjusted stock assessment models that can incorporate environmental variables, changing productivity patterns, and shifting distributions to provide more accurate projections under changing conditions. These advanced models are critical for setting appropriate harvest levels in a non-stationary environment where historical patterns may no longer provide reliable guidance.

Dynamic spatial management approaches offer important tools for responding to shifting species distributions, allowing fishery access to adjust as stocks move in response to changing environmental conditions. This approach requires both robust monitoring to track distributional shifts and flexible management frameworks that can implement spatial adjustments in a timely manner. Complementing these dynamic approaches, the identification and protection of climate refugia for vulnerable species provides strategic conservation measures for areas that may maintain suitable conditions despite broader environmental changes, potentially supporting population persistence through warming periods.

Implementation of these climate adaptation strategies faces several challenges, including data limitations, institutional barriers to flexible management, and uncertainty about specific climate impacts at species and ecosystem levels. However, the evidence clearly indicates that maintaining status quo approaches will lead to increasing management challenges and potential stock collapses as environmental conditions continue to diverge from historical patterns. Successful adaptation will require not only technical solutions but also governance innovations that can accommodate greater uncertainty, implement responsive management approaches, and coordinate actions across jurisdictional boundaries.

## 3.5 Market Integration

### 3.5.1 Market Position

The SESSF operates primarily as a supplier of fresh, high-quality seafood to domestic markets, particularly in southeastern Australia. With a gross value of production exceeding $500 million annually, representing about 23% of Australia's total fisheries production, the fishery plays a significant role in the national seafood industry. The SESSF's market position is characterized by strong product quality through fresh, local supply to major metropolitan markets, particularly Melbourne and Sydney. However, the fishery faces limitations in market diversification and value-adding capacity, creating vulnerability to import competition and changing consumer preferences.

The fishery's sustainability credentials present a mixed picture, with some well-managed stocks offering opportunities for eco-certification and premium positioning, while overfished species create reputational risks and market access challenges. This variable sustainability performance influences the fishery's ability to access premium market segments where environmental credentials are increasingly important. The multi-species nature of the SESSF creates both challenges and opportunities, allowing for diversification across different market segments but also creating complexity in marketing, quality control, and supply chain management.

Future market outlook for the SESSF appears cautiously positive with appropriate adaptive management and strategic market development. Opportunities exist for expanding domestic market share through product differentiation, sustainability certification, and enhanced consumer education about local seafood. However, competitive pressures from imports and aquaculture production will continue to challenge price points and market access, requiring ongoing innovation in quality management, value addition, and market positioning. Climate change impacts represent a significant market risk through potential effects on product availability, consistency, and quality, highlighting the importance of integrated approaches that address both resource sustainability and market development.

## 3.6 Implementation Framework

### 3.6.1 Strategic Priorities

The implementation framework for the SESSF must address multiple interconnected challenges spanning environmental, economic, management, and social dimensions. Based on the comprehensive system analysis, five top strategic priorities emerge as critical for enhancing resilience and sustainability. First, climate adaptation must be embedded throughout management systems, recognizing that south-east Australia's status as a warming hotspot creates fundamental challenges requiring systematic responses across all aspects of fishery operation. This priority encompasses monitoring enhancements, assessment modifications, and management flexibility to address shifting species distributions, changing productivity, and increasing environmental variability.

Second, ecosystem-based management approaches must be developed to move beyond traditional single-species frameworks, acknowledging the complex interactions that influence stock dynamics, particularly for non-recovering species. This priority requires multi-species harvest strategies, enhanced understanding of trophic relationships, and management approaches that can address cumulative impacts across the ecosystem. Third, market development strategies must strengthen the fishery's position in increasingly competitive seafood markets through improved product differentiation, value addition, supply chain optimization, and strategic positioning based on sustainability credentials and product quality.

Fourth, cross-jurisdictional coordination mechanisms must be established to manage stocks that traverse Commonwealth and state waters, particularly as climate change drives distributional shifts that may alter traditional management boundaries. This priority requires governance innovations, data sharing protocols, and collaborative decision-making processes that can support integrated approaches across jurisdictional lines. Fifth, socioeconomic resilience must be built within fishing communities and businesses through diversification opportunities, adaptive business models, skills development, and supportive transition arrangements during periods of necessary change. These strategic priorities are highly interconnected, requiring integrated implementation approaches that recognize their complementary nature and potential synergies.

## 3.7 Synthesis and Conclusion

The integrated systems analysis of the Southern and Eastern Scalefish and Shark Fishery reveals a complex socio-ecological system facing significant challenges from climate change, ecosystem alterations, economic pressures, and governance complexities. The key insight emerging from this integration is that addressing these challenges requires moving beyond traditional single-sector, single-species approaches toward more holistic frameworks that recognize the interconnected nature of environmental, economic, social, and governance dimensions. Climate change serves as a fundamental driver reshaping system dynamics, with south-east Australia's status as a global warming hotspot creating urgent adaptation imperatives across all aspects of fishery operation and management. The persistence of non-recovering stocks despite reduced fishing pressure highlights the limitations of conventional management approaches and the need for ecosystem-based frameworks that account for complex ecological interactions.

Critical interconnections between domains include the relationships between environmental conditions and stock productivity, management approaches and economic outcomes, technological innovations and fishing impacts, and market dynamics and operational decisions. These cross-cutting linkages create both challenges and opportunities, as interventions in one domain inevitably affect others, sometimes in unexpected ways. The multiple feedback loops operating within the system, from biological stock-recruitment relationships to economic viability dynamics and market substitution effects, further complicate management interventions and adaptation strategies. Understanding these interconnections is essential for developing coherent approaches that can enhance overall system resilience rather than optimizing individual components at the expense of system functionality.

Implementation challenges include data limitations that constrain understanding of complex ecosystem interactions, institutional barriers to cross-jurisdictional coordination, capital constraints for industry adaptation, and the inherent uncertainties of climate projections and market developments. However, opportunities exist through enhanced monitoring technologies that can provide better system information, governance innovations that can increase management responsiveness, value chain improvements that can enhance economic returns, and cross-sector integrations that can create diversification benefits. Addressing these challenges while leveraging opportunities requires collaborative approaches involving government, industry, research, indigenous communities, and broader stakeholders in developing shared understanding and coordinated responses.

Future research needs include improved understanding of ecosystem interactions affecting stock recovery, better characterization of climate change impacts at species and system levels, enhanced socioeconomic analysis of adaptation options, and more comprehensive documentation of indigenous knowledge relevant to fishery management. Particular attention should focus on understanding threshold effects and potential regime shifts that could fundamentally alter system structure and function, creating new stable states resistant to management intervention. Addressing these knowledge gaps would significantly enhance the capacity for evidence-based decision-making across all aspects of the SESSF system.

Final recommendations for the Blue Economy CRC's Futures of Seafood project emphasize the importance of integrated approaches that recognize the SESSF as a complex adaptive system requiring coordinated interventions across multiple domains. Priority should be given to enhancing adaptive capacity through improved monitoring and assessment systems, developing flexible management frameworks that can respond to changing conditions, strengthening cross-jurisdictional coordination mechanisms, supporting industry adaptation and innovation, and building resilient coastal communities with diverse economic opportunities. These integrated approaches offer the best prospect for navigating the complex challenges facing the SESSF while maintaining its significant economic, social, and cultural contributions to Australia's marine economy and coastal communities.
