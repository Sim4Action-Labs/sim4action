# Fishery System Profile: Torres Strait Lobster Fishery (TSLF)

## Brief Overview

The Torres Strait Tropical Rock Lobster Fishery (TRLF) targets the ornate rock lobster (Panulirus ornatus) and operates within the Torres Strait Protected Zone (TSPZ), extending from Cape York to the northern border of the TSPZ. This high-value fishery represents a complex social-ecological system with unique cross-jurisdictional arrangements between Australia and Papua New Guinea (PNG) under the Torres Strait Treaty. The fishery is characterized by its dual-sector structure: the Traditional Inhabitant Boat (TIB) sector (allocated 66.2% of quota) primarily composed of Torres Strait Islander fishers, and the non-Indigenous Transferable Vessel Holder (TVH) sector (allocated 33.8% of quota)[20]. Management is overseen by the Protected Zone Joint Authority (PZJA) through a quota management system established under the Torres Strait Fisheries (Quotas for Tropical Rock Lobster (Kaiar)) Management Plan 2018[20].

The ornate rock lobster exhibits a remarkable life cycle, including an annual breeding migration of up to 500 km from Torres Strait to the Gulf of Papua[5][17]. This migration pattern necessitates transboundary management coordination, with catch sharing agreed as 85% for Australian waters and 15% for PNG waters[20]. Most fishing occurs through free-diving or hookah diving methods during neap tides, with divers hand-collecting lobsters from tenders operated from small vessels[20].

The fishery represents a critical economic lifeline for many Torres Strait Islander communities while facing unique challenges including remoteness, limited infrastructure, market dependencies (primarily on Chinese live export markets), traditional rights recognition, cross-jurisdictional management complexities, and emerging climate change impacts. Recent management evolution has focused on increasing Indigenous participation, integrating traditional knowledge with western scientific management approaches, and ensuring long-term sustainability[12][20].

## System Diagram Description

The Torres Strait Lobster Fishery functions as an integrated system with multiple interconnected components. At its core is the biological subsystem centered on the ornate rock lobster's complex life cycle. Adult lobsters undertake an annual breeding migration from Torres Strait to the Gulf of Papua between November and April[5], with females carrying eggs for approximately one month before hatching into planktonic larvae that drift in oceanic currents of the northwestern Coral Sea for about six months[5]. The bifurcation of the South Equatorial Current creates distinct recruitment "source" and "sink" regions[11], with the larvae eventually developing into puerulus stage juveniles that swim across the continental shelf to settle in inshore areas, where they spend 1-2 years before moving offshore as sub-adults[5].

This biological cycle interfaces with a harvesting subsystem split between traditional inhabitant (TIB) and non-indigenous commercial (TVH) sectors[20]. The fishing activities are influenced by environmental conditions (particularly tides and water visibility)[20], seasonal patterns, fishing methods (free-diving versus hookah), and regulatory controls. Most lobsters enter the commercial fishery in their second year after hatching, with the fishery primarily targeting one and two-year-old animals, making it sensitive to annual variations in rec[text](https://www.notion.so/pysm/SIM4ACTION-DASHBOARD-b33af962a40b4604805c6937577a4e82?p%3D1106d47792548098a37ce74b86f83075%26pm%3Ds)ruitment success[8].

The management subsystem bridges biological and socioeconomic dimensions, with decision-making following a structured process involving scientific surveys, catch recommendations from advisory groups, and international negotiations with PNG[20]. The Torres Strait Treaty establishes the cross-jurisdictional framework, with catch sharing arrangements reflecting the resource distribution between Australian and PNG waters[20].

The market subsystem revolves around the high-value live export trade to China[20], creating economic dependencies but also vulnerabilities to international trade disruptions. The remoteness of Torres Strait communities introduces logistical challenges affecting handling, transport, and product quality.

The governance subsystem represents perhaps the most complex dimension, balancing western scientific management with traditional Torres Strait Islander decision-making structures and cultural practices[1][12]. This subsystem increasingly incorporates co-management approaches that blend scientific surveys with Island custom protocols[12], reflecting a gradual shift toward greater Indigenous authority and participation[9].

These subsystems interact through multiple feedback loops, creating a dynamic system where ecological, economic, social, and governance factors continuously influence each other, often with significant time delays between cause and effect.

## Primary Causal Relationships

1. **Oceanographic Patterns → Larval Dispersal → Recruitment Success**: North-western Coral Sea currents directly influence larval dispersal and subsequent recruitment dynamics in Torres Strait, with distinct regions acting as recruitment "sources" and "sinks" based on the bifurcation of the South Equatorial Current[11].

2. **Breeding Migration → Spawning Success → Future Recruitment**: The successful migration of adult lobsters from Torres Strait to breeding grounds in the Gulf of Papua (up to 500km) is critical for reproduction, with migration beginning generally in August followed by ovary development, mating, and initial oviposition occurring during migration[4][7].

3. **Environmental Conditions → Growth Rates → Time to Harvestable Size**: Growth varies significantly on both spatial (~100 km) and temporal (yearly) scales, influenced by water temperature, food availability, and density-dependent effects[13][15].

4. **Quota Management System → Fishing Effort → Harvest Levels**: The implementation of the quota management system allocates fishing rights between sectors (66.2% TIB, 33.8% TVH), directly influencing fishing effort and harvest levels[20].

5. **Treaty Obligations → Catch Sharing → Available TAC**: The Torres Strait Treaty establishes that 85% of the global TAC is to be taken in Australian waters and 15% in PNG waters, based on understanding of resource distribution, directly affecting the available catch for Australian fishers[20].

6. **Traditional Owner Participation → Management Decisions → Compliance**: Empowering Torres Strait Islanders to use Island custom alongside western management strategies has contributed to successful fishery management and improved compliance with regulations[12].

7. **Market Demand (China) → Price → Fishing Effort**: As the primary market for live lobster exports, Chinese demand fluctuations directly impact prices received by fishers, which influences fishing effort and economic returns[20].

8. **Infrastructure Limitations → Market Access → Economic Returns**: The remoteness of Torres Strait communities and limited infrastructure creates logistical challenges affecting the ability to maintain product quality and access markets, impacting economic returns[20].

9. **Scientific Surveys → Stock Assessment → TAC Setting**: Annual surveys conducted by CSIRO directly inform the stock assessment process and subsequent TAC recommendations from advisory groups[20].

10. **Post-Spawning Mortality → Population Structure → Fishing Pressure on Juveniles**: High post-spawning mortality of migratory lobsters means the fishery relies primarily on juvenile recruitment, making it sensitive to annual variations in recruitment success[4][7][8].

11. **Seasonal Conditions → Fishing Efficiency → Catch Rates**: Most fishing occurs during neap tides when currents ease and underwater visibility improves, creating a direct relationship between environmental conditions and fishing success[20].

12. **Traditional Knowledge Integration → Management Strategies → Social Acceptance**: The combination of harvest strategies developed with traditional owners, scientific surveys, and protocols informed by Island custom has supported effective management and increased social acceptance[12].

## System Dynamics

The Torres Strait Lobster Fishery exhibits several important feedback loops and non-linear relationships:

1. **Recruitment-Fishing Pressure Feedback Loop**: The fishery primarily targets one and two-year-old animals, making it highly sensitive to annual recruitment variations. When recruitment is strong, the available biomass increases, potentially leading to higher fishing pressure. However, if fishing pressure becomes too intense, it can reduce the number of adults undertaking breeding migrations, potentially affecting future recruitment[8].

2. **Growth-Density Dependence**: Research indicates temporal variations in growth rates that are likely due to density-dependent effects. Mean size of female lobsters was smaller when lobster abundance was higher, suggesting competition for resources affects growth rates[13]. This creates a balancing feedback where high population densities lead to slower growth, which can extend time to harvest size.

3. **Delayed Recruitment Response**: The six-month larval phase creates a significant time delay between breeding events and recruitment to the fishery[5]. This delay complicates management as the impacts of fishing or environmental changes on recruitment may not be immediately apparent.

4. **Market-Effort Threshold Effects**: The economic viability of fishing operations depends on prices exceeding operating costs. Given the remoteness and high operational costs in Torres Strait, there may be price thresholds below which fishing effort drops substantially, creating non-linear responses in harvest pressure.

5. **Cross-Jurisdictional Management Complexity**: The shared nature of the stock between Australia and PNG creates management interdependencies where actions in one jurisdiction affect outcomes in the other. The sequential harvesting of the population (first in Torres Strait, then during migration to PNG waters) creates particular management challenges requiring coordination[14].

6. **Traditional Knowledge-Scientific Management Integration**: There appears to be an emerging positive feedback loop where successful integration of traditional knowledge with scientific management improves outcomes, which in turn encourages further integration and co-management approaches[12].

7. **Spatial Growth Heterogeneity**: Growth rates vary significantly across the relatively small spatial scale of Torres Strait (~100 km), with lobsters in the southeastern zone generally smaller than those in the northwestern and southwestern zones[13]. This spatial heterogeneity introduces complexity in understanding and modeling population dynamics.

8. **Cultural-Economic-Ecological Balance**: The system exhibits tension between maximizing economic returns, maintaining cultural fishing practices, and ensuring ecological sustainability. These three dimensions interact in complex ways that can't be reduced to simple linear relationships.

## Management Leverage Points

1. **Recruitment Monitoring and Forecasting**: Given the fishery's high dependence on annual recruitment and the six-month larval phase, improved monitoring and forecasting of recruitment strength could provide early warnings allowing for adaptive management responses before impacts manifest in the fishery[5][8].

2. **Co-Management Framework Enhancement**: Further development of co-management approaches that meaningfully integrate traditional knowledge with scientific management represents a powerful leverage point. The success of combined Island custom and western management strategies demonstrated in other Torres Strait fisheries provides a model for expansion[12].

3. **Supply Chain Development and Diversification**: Addressing infrastructure limitations and developing alternative markets could reduce economic vulnerability to Chinese market fluctuations while increasing returns to remote communities. This represents a structural intervention with potentially significant impacts[20].

4. **Cross-Jurisdictional Coordination Mechanisms**: Strengthening the operational implementation of the Torres Strait Treaty provisions, particularly regarding the sequential harvesting of the stock during breeding migrations, could improve the management of this shared resource[14][20].

5. **Spatial Management Aligned with Growth Variation**: The significant spatial variation in growth rates[13] suggests that spatially-explicit management approaches might better optimize harvest strategies across the fishery, potentially increasing both sustainability and yields.

6. **Traditional Owner Capacity Building**: Targeted investment in building capacity among Traditional Owners for greater participation in science, monitoring, compliance, and business development could transform governance while improving socioeconomic outcomes[9][12].

7. **Quota System Refinement**: Further development of the quota management system to address specific needs and capabilities of the TIB sector could improve its effectiveness while increasing benefits to Traditional Owner communities[20].

8. **Climate Adaptation Planning**: Proactive planning for climate change impacts on the lobster resource represents an important leverage point given the potential vulnerability of recruitment patterns to changing oceanographic conditions[3][11].

9. **Data Collection Systems Enhancement**: Improved data collection, particularly from the TIB sector where reporting is currently voluntary[20], would strengthen assessment and management decision-making while potentially increasing stakeholder buy-in.

10. **Value Chain Optimization**: Interventions that increase the proportion of final market value retained within Torres Strait communities could transform economic outcomes without increasing harvest pressure on the resource.
